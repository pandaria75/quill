# Artifact Contract: Rewrite

## Purpose

The rewrite artifact records substantial revision work after review. It helps collaborators understand what changed, why it changed, and whether the article is ready for another review or final handoff.

## Recommended Sections

- **Rewrite goal**: the purpose of this revision pass.
- **Inputs considered**: review notes, updated sources, stakeholder feedback, or changed intent.
- **Major changes**: structural, factual, tonal, or scope changes made during the pass.
- **Issue resolution notes**: how blocking and non-blocking review items were handled.
- **Remaining concerns**: issues intentionally deferred, accepted risks, or areas needing another pass.
- **Reader impact**: how the rewrite better serves the audience or angle.
- **Verification notes**: claims, examples, or placeholders checked during the rewrite.
- **Recommended next step**: another review, targeted edit, final polish, or final approval.

## Guidance

The rewrite artifact should focus on meaningful changes rather than every line edit. It may be a revision log, a short companion note, or a marked-up draft summary, depending on the workflow's needs.

When review feedback is not accepted, record the rationale respectfully and plainly. This prevents the same issue from resurfacing without context and helps final approvers understand the tradeoff.

## Quality Bar

- Review blockers should be traceable to a resolution, deferral, or explicit accepted risk.
- The article's intent, angle, and structure should be more coherent after the rewrite.
- Remaining concerns should be visible before final approval.
- The rewrite notes should help reviewers focus on what changed since the last pass.

## Handoff / Relationship To Workflow

The rewrite artifact sits between review and either another draft review or finalization. It should make the revision state clear enough that collaborators can decide whether to loop back for more review or proceed toward final approval.
