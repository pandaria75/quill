# Artifact Contract: Angle

## Purpose

The angle artifact records the article's chosen framing before the outline or draft grows around it. It helps writers choose a clear promise to the reader and avoid drifting among several competing articles.

## Recommended Sections

- **Chosen angle**: the framing or thesis direction in plain language.
- **Reader promise**: what the article should help the reader understand, decide, or do.
- **Why this angle**: why this framing best serves the audience and intent.
- **Supporting tensions**: the contrast, misconception, tradeoff, or narrative question that gives the article shape.
- **Rejected alternatives**: plausible angles that were considered but set aside.
- **Evidence needs**: facts, examples, or expert input needed to support the angle.
- **Tone and stance**: whether the article should be explanatory, opinionated, practical, cautionary, introductory, or deep technical.
- **Risks**: ways the angle could overclaim, mislead, alienate the reader, or conflict with non-goals.

## Guidance

The angle should be specific enough to guide structure but flexible enough to improve during research. It may be written as a thesis, a central question, or a reader-facing promise. Prefer one primary angle with acknowledged secondary threads instead of multiple equal framings.

Rejected alternatives are useful because they protect the draft from silently absorbing old ideas. If an alternative becomes more compelling, record the reason for switching rather than blending both directions without comment.

## Quality Bar

- The chosen angle should clearly connect to the intent and audience.
- A working outline should be easier to create after reading the angle.
- Rejected alternatives should make scope boundaries more visible.
- Evidence needs and risks should reveal what later stages should verify.

## Handoff / Relationship To Workflow

The angle artifact usually follows intent and informs structure. It is a recommended input to a before-draft checkpoint. During review or rewrite, it may be revisited if reviewers find that the draft's actual argument no longer matches the intended framing.
