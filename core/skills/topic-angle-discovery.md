# Skill: Topic Angle Discovery

## Purpose

Turn a broad topic into a clear, differentiated angle worth drafting.

## When To Use

- after audience and intent are defined
- when multiple possible framings compete
- before outlining

## Inputs

- audience and intent
- source notes
- topic candidates

## Outputs

- chosen angle
- rejected alternatives with short reasons
- claim or thesis direction with its basis and uncertainty route

## Method

1. List plausible angles, including the reader problem each would clarify and the technical-blog author's intended stance.
2. Classify the proposed central claim for each angle: verified fact, evidence-backed conclusion, author judgment, assumption, inference or speculation, or unknown. Preserve the distinction between an evidence-backed angle and an explicitly opinion-led one.
3. Test each angle against audience need, available evidence, scope, and public sensitivity. An opinion-led angle may be selected when it is visibly framed as the author's judgment (for example, “我认为…”), gives its reasoning, and does not impersonate a verified consensus.
4. Reject an angle as unsupported speculation when its claim has no adequate evidence, clearly marked authorial basis, or safe path to resolution. Do not use novelty, confidence, or a smooth thesis as a substitute for support.
5. Route uncertainty. Stop and discuss with the user before selecting an angle if uncertainty could change the central claim, audience, technical conclusion, solution, factual accuracy, public sensitivity, or authorial stance. Continue only with minor uncertainty that can remain as a visible caveat or explicit marker.
6. Pick the narrowest angle that still matters, record why it wins, and state the claim basis and any caveat that must survive into the context pack and outline.

## Angle Selection Test

| Angle status | May be selected when | Required treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Evidence-supported | The evidence and reasoning support the proposed scope. | Preserve sources, limits, and the conclusion's strength. |
| Explicitly opinion-led | It is a useful author judgment, visibly marked and reasoned. | Keep it distinct from fact and consensus. |
| Assumption or inference-led | The premise is provisional but does not change a protected planning decision. | Continue only with a visible caveat and resolution point. |
| Unsupported speculation | The premise lacks evidence, a legitimate marked judgment basis, or a safe resolution path. | Do not select it; discuss or research before proceeding. |

## Question Framing And Stop Condition

When an angle is blocked, ask a short question that names the choice and its effect: “Is the article's claim that this approach is generally safer, or only that it worked in our deployment? The available evidence supports the latter; the answer changes the scope and recommendation.” Do not guess the user's desired claim or solution.

## Quality Bar

- chosen angle is specific
- angle is evidence-supported or explicitly and honestly opinion-led
- angle implies a strong structure
- assumptions, inferences, speculation, and unknowns remain visible rather than becoming thesis facts

## Common Failure Modes

- selecting a topic instead of an angle
- choosing novelty without evidence
- keeping too many equal-priority angles
- presenting a judgment or inference as a verified technical conclusion
- selecting an angle while a central claim, audience, solution, sensitivity, or stance question remains unresolved
