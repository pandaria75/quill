# Skill: Style Adaptation

## Purpose

Match tone, density, and explanation style to the intended audience and outlet while preserving the draft's factual precision and argument.

## When To Use

- after factual correction and narrative polish establish a coherent argument
- when publication context matters
- before targeted AI-flavor reduction and final editing

## Inputs

- draft
- role-card
- audience and publication expectations
- narrative-polish notes and the author's intended stance

## Outputs

- revised draft with aligned tone and authorial stance
- style notes or returned issues for later passes

## Method

1. Compare the draft's voice, technical density, and level of explanation with the intended reader and outlet.
2. Use natural first-person singular where the technical-blog author is speaking from personal experience or making a judgment. Do not force first person into every sentence or paragraph.
3. Mark opinions as opinions—for example, with a clear first-person framing—so readers can distinguish them from verified facts, evidence-backed conclusions, and unresolved speculation.
4. Adjust jargon, pacing, examples, and audience-appropriate conversational or internet-native wording. Such wording may add rapport or emphasis, but must not replace technical explanation or create material ambiguity.
5. Preserve meaning, evidence, caveats, and the author's reasoning while changing delivery. Record material style tradeoffs.
6. Pass the adapted draft to targeted AI-flavor reduction. Return it to narrative polish when the needed style change exposes a weak transition or an unclear argument; return factual or severe-ambiguity issues to factual review rather than rewriting around them.

## Quality Bar

- style fits the intended reader
- technical accuracy survives simplification
- tone stays consistent
- first-person voice and opinions feel natural rather than mechanically distributed
- conversational wording remains understandable and audience-appropriate

## Boundaries And Handoffs

- **Primary ownership:** audience fit, voice, first-person expression, explicit opinion framing, and appropriate conversational or internet-native wording.
- **Not owned here:** whether a claim is supported, whether the argument's structure works, or whether a repeated pattern is synthetic rather than intentional.
- **Hand off backward:** return factual concerns to factual review and narrative-flow concerns to narrative polish. If the wording would make a passage severely ambiguous, return it rather than accepting the ambiguity as voice.
- **Hand off forward:** AI-flavor reduction should make only targeted texture changes after style is aligned; final editing makes the final correctness and handoff decision.

## Stop Or Return Criteria

Stop when audience expectations, authorial stance, or the distinction between opinion and fact is uncertain enough that changing voice would require a guess. Do not use informal wording to conceal missing explanation, unsupported claims, or severe ambiguity.

## Common Failure Modes

- flattening useful nuance
- overfitting to a trendy voice
- changing claims while changing tone
- treating slang, fragments, or awkwardness as a required marker of authenticity
